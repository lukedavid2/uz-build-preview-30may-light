Eb chord

Three flats and a barre on the guitar. On piano it is a warm, well-balanced major. The Eb (Eb - G - Bb) is a major triad, which is why it sounds a confident major triad. Writers pick it for a singalong opening, and you can find it across pop, folk, country and rock. On guitar the chord sits easily under standard open chord shapes; on piano it stacks straight up under the right hand. The page below covers the voicings worth memorising, the theory, the progressions where the Eb earns its keep, and the records that lean on it.

The theory behind Eb

Spell out the Eb and you get Eb - G - Bb. The intervals from the root are root, major third, perfect fifth, which is the recipe for a major triad. In Bb major the chord plays the role of Subdominant (IV); in Eb major it shows up as Tonic (I). Its preferred next move is stay home or move to the IV or V, which is what makes it useful in any major-key songbook.
