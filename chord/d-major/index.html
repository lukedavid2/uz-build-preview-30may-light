D chord

Open D rings the high B and E strings, which is why folk-rock writers like Tom Petty parked here. The D (D - F# - A) is a major triad, which is why it sounds bright and stable. Writers pick it for a stable home, and you can find it across any songbook on the planet. On guitar the chord sits comfortably under a barre at the second fret; on piano it stacks straight up under the right hand. The page below covers the voicings worth memorising, the theory, the progressions where the D earns its keep, and the records that lean on it.

The theory behind D

Spell out the D and you get D - F# - A. The intervals from the root are root, major third, perfect fifth, which is the recipe for a major triad. In G major the chord plays the role of Dominant (V); in D major it shows up as Tonic (I). Its preferred next move is stay home or move to the IV or V, which is what makes it useful in any major-key songbook.
