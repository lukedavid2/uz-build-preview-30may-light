Simple open-chord embellishments

A major chord is built from the 1, 3 and 5 of the major scale. A minor chord is built from the 1, b3 and 5. An embellishment moves one of those chord notes up or down on the same string, landing on a different scale degree that produces a fuller, more colourful chord. Most of the safe moves are a single fret or two. This page shows all eight foundational open chords with one embellishment each as a quick reference; click any chord for the full vocabulary.

Safe vs risky additions

The safe additions live inside the chord's scale and create smooth voice-leading. The risky additions change the chord's function or step outside the scale.

Why these moves work

Every safe embellishment shown here lives inside the chord's own scale. That means it cannot clash with the original chord tones: it just adds a new colour, a brighter, darker or more open voicing of the same underlying harmony. The 2 and the 6 are gentle additions that don't change the chord's function. The 4 sets up a suspension that pulls back to the 3. The 7 in a major chord softens the tonic into a maj7; the b7 in a minor chord is its natural seventh.

The risky moves all touch the chord's function or step outside its scale. The b7 over a major chord turns it into a dominant, and dominants want to resolve, so they break stasis. The #4 lives in Lydian and floats. In a minor chord the natural 6 and major 7 sit in Dorian and harmonic minor respectively. Used briefly, they sound modal and beautiful; held too long, they erase the chord's centre.

Make a song out of them

Most of the songs you know already use embellishments without naming them. The trick is to apply them deliberately: pick one chord in a progression and embellish it once a bar, or cycle through two embellishments over a single chord for an entire verse. The interactive chord builder on the home page is a good place to audition what each move sounds like in context; RhymeForge and CollisionLab handle the lyric side.
