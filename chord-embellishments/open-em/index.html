Embellishing the open Em chord

The open E minor chord is built from 1, b3, 5. An embellishment moves one of those chord tones up or down on the same string to land on a different scale degree, producing a fuller, more colourful version that still resolves cleanly back home. This page shows the safe and risky single-finger moves you can make from the standard Em shape.

Safe embellishments · 2, 4, b7

The safe additions for a minor chord are the 2, 4, b7 of the scale: they belong to natural minor and don't disturb the chord's gravity. Each of the 3 variants below changes a single note on a single string from the base shape.

Em(add9)
+2

Lift the D string from E up to F#, the 2 of E minor. Em(add9) sounds spacious and a little melancholy. A signature colour of cinematic minor-key writing.

Emsus4
+4

Fret the G string at 2 instead of open: A, the 4. The b3 disappears and you get a hovering suspension that resolves beautifully back to Em.

Em7
+b7

Open the D string for D natural, the b7. Em7 is mellow and modal, the gateway chord of mid-tempo acoustic ballads in the key of G or D.

Risky embellishments · 6, 7

The risky additions are the 6, 7: they introduce a brighter or major-seventh colour that can break the minor mood if you linger. Use them as passing colours rather than parking spots, and they will earn their keep.

Em6
+6

Fret the B string at 2 for C#, the natural 6 of E minor. Em6 has a sultry, slightly noir quality and works best in a deliberately moody setting where the bright 6 over the dark b3 makes sense.

Em(maj7)
+7

Pull the D string down a fret to D#, the major 7. Em(maj7) is the sound of a Bond theme or a vintage spy soundtrack: tense, glamorous, unstable. Use it briefly.

Why these moves work

The safe embellishments for Em sit inside either natural minor or Dorian. The 2 and 4 are bright colours that don't disturb the minor centre. The b7 is the chord's natural seventh, the calling card of the minor 7 sound. Together they form the vocabulary of acoustic-pop minor playing.

The risky moves borrow from outside the natural minor. The 6 is the Dorian colour: it brightens the chord in a way that can sound modal or jazzy. The major 7 makes the minor maj7 chord, glamorous and tense. Both work in moderation; linger and you lose the song's centre.

How to practise

Start by gripping the standard E minor shape and strumming four times. Then apply one embellishment at a time: hold the new shape for four strums, return to the base for four, and repeat. Get the muscle memory before you try to fit the move into a song. Once each embellishment feels easy on its own, try cycling two of them inside the same bar, for example base to add9 to base to sus4. That little four-step cycle is the engine of an enormous amount of acoustic guitar writing.
