Chord progressions in E major

Built around E major, this is the rock and blues home key, the lowest open string on a guitar. The easy steadiness of the tonic chord pulls a listener in before the progression has started. The low E string of a guitar is the lowest natural root chord, so the whole instrument resonates here. The diatonic chords, four to six of the progressions that account for most songs ever written in E major, and a short note on which borrowed colours push the harmony sideways without breaking it are all laid out in the sections below.

The pop axis
E - B - C#m - A

The four-chord engine behind a thousand pop hits. The lift from I to V opens the chorus, vi pulls down into feeling, IV walks back toward home.

Borrowed chords for E major

Chords pulled in from the parallel key to add colour. These four are the ones songwriters reach for most often when E major starts to feel too plain.

D

The sound of classic rock backsteps. Pulls the harmony briefly into the parallel minor before resolving home.

Am
From parallel minor (iv)

Replaces the bright IV with its minor twin. A short minor iv in a major progression creates the famous bittersweet pivot.

C

A surprise major chord a step below the dominant. Used in bridges to push the listener into a new emotional room.

G
From parallel minor (bIII)

A rich, unexpected major chord built on the flattened third. A favourite of indie and dream-pop writers.

Why songwriters reach for E major

Writers pick E major when they want the home key of blues and rock for guitar players because of that low E open chord, and the practical side of the choice is this: four sharps, with the open low E gives a guitar its lowest natural root chord; piano begins to flex with the four sharps. Guitar players will find that the open shapes that fit this key make strumming fluid. Piano players will find that the keyboard layout sits well under most players' hands. The trade-off is straightforward. E major buys you the home key of blues and rock for guitar players because of that low E open chord at the cost of a slightly trickier transposition map.
