Chord progressions in E minor

E minor is the open-string guitar minor that powers half of rock and folk. Songwriters reach for it when they want slow-burn tension without drowning a melody, leaning on its diatonic chords to support a quieter vocal. Open Em is the easiest grip on a guitar, which is why so much folk-rock and metal lives here. The page below collects the seven scale chords with Roman numerals, the named progressions that turn up most often, and the borrowed chords that surprise the ear with a major chord when the home key starts to feel small.

Common progressions in E minor

Six patterns that show up again and again in songs written in this key. The chord names below are spelled out in E minor so you can drop them straight into a verse or chorus.

i - VI - III - VII
The minor anthem
Em - C - G - D

Heroic minor four-chord. The descent from i to VI to III gives the verse weight, VII slingshots back to the tonic. The Andalusian cousin of the pop axis.

Used in: 'Save Tonight' by Eagle-Eye Cherry, 'Zombie' by The Cranberries, 'Numb' by Linkin Park

i - VII - VI - VII
The modal lament
Em - D - C - D

Three chords down, one back up. Drone-like and modal, common in folk-rock and Britpop. The VII to VI walk gives a wistful sigh.

Used in: 'Stairway to Heaven' by Led Zeppelin (intro), 'All Along the Watchtower' by Bob Dylan, 'House of the Rising Sun' by The Animals

i - iv - V - i
Harmonic minor cadence
Em - Am - Bm - Em

The classical pull. The V is borrowed from harmonic minor (a major V instead of v), creating a sharper push back to the tonic. Used in flamenco, classical and metal alike.

Used in: 'Hit the Road Jack' by Ray Charles, 'Black Magic Woman' by Santana, 'Sultans of Swing' by Dire Straits

i - VI - VII - i
Andalusian climb
Em - C - D - Em

A relative of the descending Andalusian cadence, reordered. Opens dark, lifts through VI and VII, lands firm. Sounds Spanish but turns up in metal and trap.

Used in: 'Smooth' by Santana feat. Rob Thomas, 'Sweet Dreams' by Eurythmics, 'Hotel California' by Eagles

i - III - VII - VI
The cinematic minor
Em - G - D - C

Spacious and filmic. The III chord is the relative major, providing a brief lift before the song descends again. A staple of trailer music and indie ballads.

Used in: 'Mad World' by Tears for Fears, 'Bad Romance' by Lady Gaga, 'Radioactive' by Imagine Dragons

i - iv - VII - III
Cycle-of-fifths minor
Em - Am - D - G

Each chord pulls cleanly to the next around the cycle of fifths. Used in jazz minor turnarounds and many Latin progressions.

Used in: 'Hit the Road Jack' by Ray Charles, 'Wake Me Up When September Ends' by Green Day, 'I Will Survive' by Gloria Gaynor

Borrowed chords for E minor

Chords pulled in from the parallel key to add colour. These four are the ones songwriters reach for most often when E minor starts to feel too plain.

B
From harmonic minor (V)

Replaces the natural v with a major V chord. The leading tone in the V gives a much stronger pull back to the tonic.

A
From parallel major (IV)

The bright IV inside a minor key. A common move in modal pop and folk that hints at the parallel major without committing.

E
From parallel major (Picardy)

Ending a minor song on the major tonic. A classical trick (the Picardy third) still used in jazz and gospel for surprise resolution.

F#m
From parallel major (ii)

Borrowed as a passing colour. Sits a half-step above the diatonic ii° and feels less sour, opening a smoother turnaround.

Why songwriters reach for E minor

Writers pick E minor when they want the dominant key of folk and rock minor songs because Em is the easiest open chord on the guitar, and the practical side of the choice is this: one sharp (F#), with open Em is the friendliest chord on the guitar; piano sits in a strong register. Guitar players will find that the open shapes that fit this key make strumming fluid. Piano players will find that the keyboard layout sits well under most players' hands. The trade-off is straightforward. E minor buys you the dominant key of folk and rock minor songs because Em is the easiest open chord on the guitar at the cost of a slightly trickier transposition map.
