Chord progressions in F# major

F# major is an enharmonic cousin of Gb, popular in jazz, gospel and big-band. Songwriters reach for it when they want uncluttered momentum without going flat, leaning on its diatonic chords to let a hook breathe. Most print sources spell it as Gb, but the sounding pitches are identical. The page below collects the seven scale chords with Roman numerals, the named progressions that turn up most often, and the borrowed chords that give a bridge somewhere new to go when the home key starts to feel small.

The four-chord engine behind a thousand pop hits. The lift from I to V opens the chorus, vi pulls down into feeling, IV walks back toward home.

Borrowed chords for F# major

Chords pulled in from the parallel key to add colour. These four are the ones songwriters reach for most often when F# major starts to feel too plain.

The sound of classic rock backsteps. Pulls the harmony briefly into the parallel minor before resolving home.

Replaces the bright IV with its minor twin. A short minor iv in a major progression creates the famous bittersweet pivot.

A surprise major chord a step below the dominant. Used in bridges to push the listener into a new emotional room.

A rich, unexpected major chord built on the flattened third. A favourite of indie and dream-pop writers.

Why songwriters reach for F# major

F# major carries six sharps, which means all chords are barred on guitar; pianists treat it as the same fingering as Gb. Writers gravitate to it for more commonly seen as Gb in print. On guitar, barre chords are unavoidable, but a capo can move the music into open-string territory. A capo at the second or third fret can shift the shape repertoire while keeping the key. On piano, piano players find the hand position natural. The most common reason to pick F# major over a neighbour is the colour: but used in gospel, jazz and modulated big-band charts.
