Chord progressions in C major

Built around C major, this is the no-accidentals starting point. The easy steadiness of the tonic chord pulls a listener in before the progression has started. Most music-theory books open here for a reason, and most singer-songwriters return to it for the same reason. The diatonic chords, four to six of the progressions that account for most songs ever written in C major, and a short note on which borrowed colours give a bridge somewhere new to go are all laid out in the sections below.

Common progressions in C major

Six patterns that show up again and again in songs written in this key. The chord names below are spelled out in C major so you can drop them straight into a verse or chorus.

I - V - vi - IV
The pop axis
C - G - Am - F

The four-chord engine behind a thousand pop hits. The lift from I to V opens the chorus, vi pulls down into feeling, IV walks back toward home.

Used in: 'Don't Stop Believin'' by Journey, 'Let It Be' by The Beatles, 'With or Without You' by U2

vi - IV - I - V
Sad pop, optimistic chorus
Am - F - C - G

Same chords as the pop axis, started on the relative minor. The minor opening gives the verse weight before the chorus climbs.

Used in: 'Apologize' by OneRepublic, 'Grenade' by Bruno Mars, 'Someone Like You' by Adele

ii - V - I
The jazz turnaround
Dm - G - C

The cornerstone of every jazz standard. ii sets up the dominant, V resolves home with full gravity. Add a seventh on each chord for the canonical sound.

Used in: 'Autumn Leaves' (standard), 'Fly Me to the Moon' by Frank Sinatra, 'All the Things You Are' (standard)

I - IV - V
The three-chord workhorse
C - F - G

The bedrock of rock, blues and country. Three chords, every song. Simple to play, hard to make sound fresh.

Used in: 'Twist and Shout' by The Beatles, 'La Bamba' by Ritchie Valens, 'Wild Thing' by The Troggs

I - vi - IV - V
Fifties doo-wop
C - Am - F - G

The doo-wop bedrock. Stable on I, drift down to vi, climb back via IV and V. Used flat-out for ballads in every decade since.

Used in: 'Stand By Me' by Ben E. King, 'Earth Angel' by The Penguins, 'Every Breath You Take' by The Police

I - V - IV - I
Country cadence
C - G - F - C

A confident verse pattern that walks out and walks home in four bars. Common in country, folk and 90s alt-rock.

Used in: 'Brown Eyed Girl' by Van Morrison, 'Wagon Wheel' by Old Crow Medicine Show, 'Ring of Fire' by Johnny Cash

Borrowed chords for C major

Chords pulled in from the parallel key to add colour. These four are the ones songwriters reach for most often when C major starts to feel too plain.

Bb
From parallel minor (bVII)

The sound of classic rock backsteps. Pulls the harmony briefly into the parallel minor before resolving home.

Fm
From parallel minor (iv)

Replaces the bright IV with its minor twin. A short minor iv in a major progression creates the famous bittersweet pivot.

Ab
From parallel minor (bVI)

A surprise major chord a step below the dominant. Used in bridges to push the listener into a new emotional room.

Eb
From parallel minor (bIII)

A rich, unexpected major chord built on the flattened third. A favourite of indie and dream-pop writers.

Why songwriters reach for C major

C major carries no sharps or flats, which means white keys only on piano, all natural notes on guitar. Writers gravitate to it for the default classroom key. On guitar, the open shapes that fit this key make strumming fluid. A capo at the second or third fret can shift the shape repertoire while keeping the key. On piano, the keyboard layout sits well under most players' hands. The most common reason to pick C major over a neighbour is the colour: the cleanest playground for ear training, and the easiest place to learn a new song before transposing.
