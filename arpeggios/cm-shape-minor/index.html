The Cm-shape minor arpeggio

The C-shape arpeggio with the third flatted to a b3.

The Cm-shape minor arpeggio sits in the first seven frets, anchored on the A string at fret 3 (the root, C). The flat third (Eb) lives on the B string at fret 4 and on the high-e string at fret 4. The perfect fifth (G) lives on the open G string and on the D string at fret 5. The only difference from the C-shape major arpeggio is the third: Eb instead of E, one fret lower on each string where the third appears.

Why this shape

The Cm-shape arpeggio shares its skeleton with the C-shape major. The root and the fifth stay put. Only the third moves down one fret. That single-fret swap is the entire difference between major and minor, and it is one of the most useful things to internalise on the guitar.

The Cm-shape connects up the neck to the Am-shape, just as the C-shape connects to the A-shape. The same shared-note logic applies: the Am-shape root on the A string at fret 12 is the octave above the Cm-shape root at fret 3, so you can hop between the two shapes without losing the root note in your ear.

How to practise

Three short drills that lock the Cm-shape minor shape into your hands. Three minutes each, total nine minutes a day, and the shape is yours in a fortnight.

1

Isolate the shape

Play all chord tones ascending then descending. Keep the b3 distinct in your ear: it is the colour note of the shape, the reason the chord is sad. Three minutes at 60-80bpm.

2

Connect to the Am-shape

Play the Cm-shape ascending up the neck. Without a break, slide into the Am-shape at fret 12 descending. Loop for three minutes; the shared root makes the connection feel like one big shape.

3

Four-bar improvisation

Loop a C minor chord. Improvise a four-bar phrase using only the Cm-shape. Try resolving each phrase on the b3 (Eb) rather than the root: it is the most minor-sounding landing point.

Where it shows up

The Cm-shape arpeggio is the home of melancholic fingerpicking in C minor. It also turns up constantly in pop songs in Eb major (where C minor is the relative minor) as the arpeggio under the vi chord. Bon Iver, Sufjan Stevens and Phoebe Bridgers all live in this position when they want a brooding open-string verse with a melancholic edge.
