The Am-shape minor arpeggio

The barred Am-shape arpeggio, rooted on the A string.

The Am-shape minor arpeggio is the most-played minor arpeggio in rock, blues and jazz, because the Am barre chord is the most-played minor barre. For C minor it lives at fret 3. The root is on the A string at fret 3 and on the G string at fret 5. The perfect fifth lives on the D string at fret 5. The flat third (the colour note) sits on the B string at fret 4. The shape is compact, ergonomic, and easy to memorise because it matches a chord shape you already grip.

Why this shape

The Am-shape arpeggio has its root on the A string, which makes it the easiest minor shape to use for ear-training. Hum the root, find it on the A string, then play the arpeggio. The shape covers a full octave between the two roots (A string fret 3 to G string fret 5), which is enough range for most fills without leaving position.

The Am-shape sits between the Cm-shape (down the neck) and the Gm-shape (up the neck). The connection to the Cm-shape is the smoothest in the CAGED system: both shapes share the A string root at fret 3 and the D string fifth at fret 5.

How to practise

Three short drills that lock the Am-shape minor shape into your hands. Three minutes each, total nine minutes a day, and the shape is yours in a fortnight.

1

Isolate the shape

Play all chord tones ascending then descending. Three minutes at 60-80bpm. The b3 on the B string is the heart of the shape; let it ring slightly longer than the other notes to internalise the minor colour.

2

Connect to the Cm-shape

Play the Am-shape ascending. Without a break, slide down to the Cm-shape and play it descending. The shared A-string root at fret 3 is the pivot. Loop for three minutes.

3

Four-bar improvisation

Loop a C minor chord. Improvise a four-bar phrase using only the Am-shape. Try targeting the b3 on the B string for the most minor-sounding resolutions, then the root on the A string for the most grounded resolutions.

Where it shows up

The Am-shape arpeggio shows up constantly in minor-key indie and folk. Any song in C minor (or relative Eb major) with a picked verse intro is usually using this shape. Producers love the centred mid-range of this shape for chorused clean tones, and jazz comping pianists translating to guitar tend to default here because the b3 is right under the middle finger and the root is right under the index.
