The E-shape major arpeggio

The classic barre arpeggio, rooted on the low-E.

The E-shape major arpeggio is the most-used CAGED shape on guitar, because the E barre chord is the most-used barre. For C major it lives at fret 8, with roots on the low-E, D and high-e strings. The third (E) sits on the G string at fret 9 and on the low-E and high-e at fret 12. The shape feels symmetrical and compact, and it is the first arpeggio most rock and blues guitarists ever learn.

Why this shape

The E-shape arpeggio is built around the most familiar grip on the guitar, the barre E chord. That familiarity is its biggest strength: you already know where every chord tone is because you have been gripping them for years. The root on the low-E lets you find any major arpeggio just by walking up the neck.

The E-shape sits between the G-shape (down the neck) and the D-shape (further up). Connecting it down to the G-shape is one of the smoothest position shifts in CAGED. The E-shape low-E root at fret 8 is the same note as the G-shape's low-E root, so you can hover between them by sliding only one finger.

How to practise

Three short drills that lock the E-shape major shape into your hands. Three minutes each, total nine minutes a day, and the shape is yours in a fortnight.

1

Isolate the shape

Play all chord tones inside the shape ascending then descending. Keep the index barre relaxed at fret 8; the barre is a finger rest, not a clamp. Three minutes at 60-80bpm.

2

Connect to the G-shape

Play the E-shape descending into the G-shape below. The two shapes share notes on every string, so the transition is more of a slide than a jump. Loop the connection for three minutes.

3

Four-bar improvisation

Loop a C major chord. Improvise a four-bar phrase using only the E-shape arpeggio. Try targeting the major third on the G string fret 9 for resolution; it is the sweetest landing in the shape.

Where it shows up

The E-shape arpeggio is the workhorse of rock leads, blues fills and country breaks. Almost every guitar solo in a major key uses this shape as its home base. For songwriters the E-shape is the shape to reach for when you want a riff that doubles a vocal line, or a melodic intro figure that decorates a barre chord without leaving the position. Lindsey Buckingham lives here.
