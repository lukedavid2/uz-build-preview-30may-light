The D-shape major arpeggio

The smallest CAGED shape, root on the D string.

The D-shape major arpeggio is the smallest and most compact of the five CAGED shapes. For C major it sits around fret 10, with roots on the D string at fret 10 and the B string at fret 13. Only four of the six strings carry chord tones in this shape, which makes it the most economical shape to play but also the trickiest to navigate without context: there are no roots on the low strings to anchor your ear.

Why this shape

The D-shape arpeggio compresses the chord onto the upper four strings. The root on the D string is the lowest chord tone in the shape, which is unusual: most CAGED shapes give you a root on the low-E or A string for a strong bass anchor. The trade-off is brightness: this shape is the highest-sounding of the five, and it suits chord-melody and counter-melody work where you want the chord tones to sing above the rest of the arrangement.

The D-shape sits between the E-shape (down the neck) and the C-shape (the next octave up). The link to the E-shape is particularly clean: the E-shape root on the high-e at fret 8 connects through to the D-shape root on the B string at fret 13 with a single position shift.

How to practise

Three short drills that lock the D-shape major shape into your hands. Three minutes each, total nine minutes a day, and the shape is yours in a fortnight.

1

Isolate the shape

Play the four chord tones in the shape ascending then descending. The shape is small, so this is a stamina drill: keep going for three minutes at 70-90bpm and notice how much tighter your right hand has to be for the small intervals.

2

Connect to the E-shape

Play the D-shape descending into the E-shape at fret 8. The two shapes share notes on the B and high-e strings, so the slide down is almost seamless. Loop for three minutes.

3

Four-bar improvisation

Loop a C major chord. Improvise a four-bar phrase using only the D-shape. Treat the shape as a chord-melody scaffold: think 'what would the singer's harmony line do here?' and play that.

Where it shows up

The D-shape arpeggio is the secret weapon of chord-melody players. Because all four notes sit on the top strings, the shape works beautifully under a sung melody without crowding the vocal register. Use it for the bridge of a song in C when you want a sudden lift in brightness, or as a counter-melody behind another instrument's lead. Singer-songwriters often park here for the final chorus.
