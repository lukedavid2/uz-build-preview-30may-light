The Em-shape minor arpeggio

The classic minor barre arpeggio, rooted on the low-E.

The Em-shape minor arpeggio is the most-used CAGED minor shape, because the Em barre is the most-used minor barre. For C minor it lives at fret 8. Roots are on the low-E, D and high-e at fret 8. The flat third (Eb) sits on the low-E at fret 11, the G string at fret 8 and the high-e at fret 11. The perfect fifth (G) is on the A and B strings at fret 10 and fret 8 and on the G string at fret 12. The shape is symmetrical and compact.

Why this shape

The Em-shape arpeggio is built around the most familiar minor grip on the guitar. That familiarity is the shape's strength: you already know where every chord tone lives because you have been gripping them since the first time you played a barre Em. The roots on the low-E let you find any minor arpeggio just by walking the position up or down.

The Em-shape sits between the Gm-shape (down the neck) and the Dm-shape (further up). The connection down to the Gm-shape is one of the smoothest in CAGED: the two shapes share notes on every string, so the slide down feels less like a position shift and more like a stretch.

How to practise

Three short drills that lock the Em-shape minor shape into your hands. Three minutes each, total nine minutes a day, and the shape is yours in a fortnight.

1

Isolate the shape

Play all chord tones ascending then descending. Three minutes at 60-80bpm. The shape has three notes per string in places; let your fretting hand sit at the centre and let the index and pinky reach out.

2

Connect to the Gm-shape

Play the Em-shape descending into the Gm-shape at fret 5-10. The shared notes mean the transition is almost a slide rather than a jump. Loop for three minutes.

3

Four-bar improvisation

Loop a C minor chord. Improvise a four-bar phrase using only the Em-shape. Try landing on the b3 (Eb) on the G string fret 8 for resolution: it is the sweetest minor landing in the shape.

Where it shows up

The Em-shape arpeggio is the workhorse of minor-key rock leads, blues riffs and metal solos. Almost every guitar solo in a minor key uses this shape as its home base. For songwriters it is the shape to reach for when you want a riff that doubles a vocal line in a minor verse, or a melodic intro figure that decorates a barre Em or Am chord without leaving the position.
