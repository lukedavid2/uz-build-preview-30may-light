The C-shape major arpeggio

The arpeggio that anchors on the open C major chord.

The C-shape major arpeggio sits in the first five frets, wrapped around the open C chord. The root is on the A string at fret 3, the major third is one fret below on the D string, the perfect fifth is on the open G string, and the high-e open delivers another major third for free. Because three of the six chord tones come off open strings, this is the first arpeggio shape most fingerstyle guitarists ever learn, and the one folk songs in C lean on most heavily.

Why this shape

The C-shape arpeggio is the only CAGED shape where the root sits on the A string and the open high-e string carries a chord tone. That free top E is why folk and indie songwriters in C reach for this shape so often: a single picked low note plus a brushed open e already outlines a C chord with no left hand needed.

Travel up the neck and the next CAGED shape is the A-shape, sitting around fret 12 with its root on the A string at the octave above. Play the C-shape ascending, then slide the same hand position up the neck and you land in the A-shape: the two shapes share three common notes, which is what gives CAGED its name and its connective power.

How to practise

Three short drills that lock the C-shape major shape into your hands. Three minutes each, total nine minutes a day, and the shape is yours in a fortnight.

1

Isolate the shape

Pick all six arpeggio notes ascending then descending at 60bpm for one minute. Then bump to 70bpm for another minute. Then 80bpm. Three minutes total. The aim is clean, even notes with the open strings ringing fully.

2

Connect to the A-shape

Play the C-shape ascending up to fret 5. Without a break, slide your hand up the neck and play the A-shape (around fret 12) descending. Loop for three minutes. The connection should feel like one long line, not two patterns glued together.

3

Four-bar improvisation

Loop a C major chord on a recorder or backing track. Over it, improvise a singable four-bar phrase using only the notes of this shape. Keep the rhythm simple. Repeat until something memorable lands; that fragment is the seed of a melody.

Where it shows up

The C-shape arpeggio is the home territory of folk fingerpicking in C major. Drop the thumb on the A string at fret 3, let the open G and high-e ring, and you already have three of the four notes of a Cmaj7. Indie pop intros that loop a C chord for four bars almost always pick the notes of this shape in some order, often as a Travis pattern or a delicate one-note-per-beat arpeggio. Joni Mitchell, Nick Drake and Sufjan Stevens all live here.
