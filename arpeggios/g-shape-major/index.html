The G-shape major arpeggio

The G-shape arpeggio, root on the low-E and high-e.

The G-shape major arpeggio is the widest CAGED shape: it spans five frets and frames the entire neck region between the A-shape below and the E-shape above. For C major it sits around frets 5-10, with roots on the low-E, D and high-e strings at fret 8, fret 10 and fret 8 respectively. The wide stretch is what makes this shape the favourite of soloists who want to cover both the low and high ranges in a single position.

Why this shape

The G-shape arpeggio is the one CAGED shape that has roots on three different strings (low-E, D and high-e). That triple-octave anchor makes it ideal for two-handed tapping figures, sweep patterns and any line that wants to cover three octaves quickly. The trade-off is the wide stretch, which can take time to develop.

The G-shape sits between the A-shape (down the neck) and the E-shape (further up). The link from the A-shape to the G-shape is the smallest jump in the CAGED system: the A-shape's high-e C at fret 8 is the same note as the G-shape's high-e root, so the two shapes literally touch on that string.

How to practise

Three short drills that lock the G-shape major shape into your hands. Three minutes each, total nine minutes a day, and the shape is yours in a fortnight.

1

Isolate the shape

Play all chord tones in the shape ascending then descending. The wide stretch matters most on the lower strings; let your fretting hand sit at the second finger and let the index reach back. Three minutes at 60-80bpm.

2

Connect to the A-shape

Play the G-shape descending into the A-shape below. The G-shape low-E root at fret 8 connects to the A-shape A-string root at fret 3 via a clean position shift. Loop the connection for three minutes.

3

Four-bar improvisation

Loop a C major chord. Improvise a four-bar phrase that uses both the lowest and the highest octave of the shape. The wide range is the point: take advantage of it instead of staying in the middle.

Where it shows up

The G-shape arpeggio is the home of rock soloists working in C major. Its wide reach lets a single hand position cover three octaves, which is why so many lead guitarists park here for the verse and switch only when the chord changes. For songwriters the G-shape is useful when you want a lead line that sounds spacious and orchestral: the triple-octave roots make even a simple melody feel layered.
